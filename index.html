Natural disasters have always been a part of weather phenomena, but lately, the number and severity of these events seemed to have increased tremendously. But wait, before you cite the two c-words as the source of this calamity, let’s step back and talk about weather.

Weather

Weather, defined by the National Centers for Environmental Information, is the “mix of events that happen each day in our atmosphere” (1). Weather can change within mere minutes and includes phenomena like rain, wind, and snow. A single instance of a natural disaster would fall under the weather category.

But it seems like we’ve had more of these storms recently, right? The term “weather” is used to describe what happens in a given instance, not justify long-term trends…

Climate

Climate is “what the weather is like over a long period of time in a specific area” (1). So the weather that an area usually experiences is considered the climate of that location.

In order to determine the climate characteristics of a region, scientists take measurements of different variables such as temperature, precipitation, wind, cloud cover, humidity, and even aerosols over a period of time (2). There is a such thing as global climate: the average weather conditions for the entire planet over a certain period of time (1).

Although there are more nuances to how these variables are used for calculations, typically averages are used. For example, rainfall records are taken over a year to calculate the annual precipitation levels of an area, which characterizes the climate based on how dry or wet the region is.

Let’s use the variable temperature for some demonstrations.

However, there’s an important question that should be asked: how was the temperature data collected in the first place?

The history of measuring fluctuations in temperature goes all the way back to 1610, when Galileo made a thermoscope using a glass sphere containing alcohols each with different densities (3). The alcohols would rise and fall within the container based on the ambient temperature, but this device didn’t have any markings to determine the exact temperature, hence why it’s not considered a “thermometer” (3). In 1612, Santorio Santorio made a thermoscope calibrated to measure human temperature, and in 1654, Ferdinand II de’ Medici made a thermometer “to depend on the expansion and contraction of a liquid, which was independent of barometric pressure” (3).

The first set of continuous temperature readings from thermometers comes from the Central England Temperature Data Series, which started in 1659 (4). Though, we don’t have substantial data on global temperatures before the 1880s.

Before digitalized thermometers, scientists typically used thermometers that contained some liquid -- often mercury -- within glass (15). However, there were other types of thermometers, such as thermistors, that scientists used for weather reports (15). Although liquid thermometers and thermistors would both theoretically measure the same temperature, they both do so using different processes, so there’s a valid question on how temperature data can be affected by this variation.

There are also different processes and methodologies that were used to collect temperature data. Some thermometers were contained in Cotton Region Shelters, little wooden boxes that would shade the device (15). Other thermometers were exposed to direct sunlight. Different weather stations also collected their data at different times of the day while others used a maximum-minimum temperature system (15).

There’s also the heat island effect or the urban heat island, where urbanized areas are warmer than surrounding rural areas (16). Again, a weather station located in the heart of a bustling city and another station a few dozen miles away from the metropole can produce different data sets for the atmospheric temperature.

So it looks like temperature data can get skewed by the type of thermometer used and the location of the device in relation to sunlight and urban spaces.

However, scientists have accounted for these variations after collecting weather station data and before making climate modules. This consideration can involve combing through data to find inconsistencies, such as unnatural spikes in temperature readings, or creating algorithms to smooth out data points (17)(18). The math behind these standardizations is pretty complex, but the overall process is strategically minimizing any abnormal variations that have arisen from data collection methods to ensure the data accurately portrays a region’s characteristics.

Though after all these adjustments, there still seems to be a steady increase in temperatures by around 1°C. So what could be causing this uptick?

One cause of temperature changes is based on the chemical composition of the atmosphere. There are certain gases, such as carbon dioxide and methane, that effectively traps heat within the surface and lower atmospheric levels.

As you may already know, scientists use the term “greenhouse gases” to categorize them due to their heat-trapping characteristics like a greenhouse. It seems like these gases have the potential to warm up the atmosphere, but are they actually causing the rising temperature trend we’re experiencing?

Well to determine this, scientists have gathered past samples of air from drilling into ice cores. When ice freezes, little air pockets that contain the chemical composition of the atmosphere at the time get trapped within. Over several thousands of years, different layers of ice form, where each level corresponds to different time periods. Scientists drill and extract huge ice cores, and carefully taking air samples from a layer allows scientists to determine the atmospheric composition of a long time ago. In fact, air samples from 800 thousand years ago have been recovered!

So there has been an increase in these types of gases in recent years, but what exactly is their source? After all, the Earth has massive carbon sinks, several organisms produce carbon dioxide through cellular respiration, and CO2 is naturally released through volcanic eruptions and forest fires (19).

Before we answer that question, let’s just talk about carbon dioxide, specifically the structure of carbon atoms. All carbon atoms have 6 protons (that’s what makes them a carbon atom!) but can have differing numbers of neutrons. Atoms that have the same number of protons but vary in their neutron count are called isotopes. Most carbon atoms have 6 neutrons, but others can have 7 or even 8 neutrons, and these are all considered different isotopes of carbon. Carbon atoms with 6 neutrons are abbreviated as 12C, and carbon atoms with 7 or 8 neutrons are abbreviated as 13C or 14C, respectively. 12C is the lightest isotope while 14C is the heaviest. While the other two isotopes are stable, 14C is radioactive (6). This means that over time, 14C will turn into 14N through the process of beta minus decay. Essentially, a neutron within the carbon atom transforms into a proton, releasing an electron and an antineutrino in the process (5).

Although we see rising CO2 levels in terms of ppm, the concentration of measured 14C has actually been decreasing over the past decade or so (10). Air samples from the terrestrial biosphere, ocean, and general atmosphere -- which are all considered carbon sinks -- have similar 14C concentration values, so carbon released from these sources won’t affect the ratio of 14C within the air (9)(10).

However, we do know that fossil fuels don’t have 14C because any 14C atom within the original ancient plant would have decayed by the present day (8). So burning fossil fuels, which contain a significant concentration of 12C, is changing the composition of the atmosphere!

This makes sense because the 1760s was the advent of the Industrial Revolution. During this era, coal was used to power steam engines and make steel through a process of smelting coal with iron (12). Coal is considered a fossil fuel because it’s produced from “the remains of dead plants and animals” within the Earth’s crust (12).

The Industrial Revolution began in Britain and later came to other now-first-world countries such as the US. This era revolutionized people’s lives through the number of new developments people now had access to, including forms of transportation and electricity. Subsequently, the demand and usage for fossil fuels dramatically increased around the 1950s due to more people purchasing cars, using electricity, and manufacturing steel products (13). Ever since, we across the globe have still been mostly using fossil fuels to power our homes, cars, and our lifestyles.

As we can see, an increase in fossil fuel consumption corresponds well with an uptick in global temperatures!

Although we don’t depend as heavily on coal nowadays compared to the mid-1900s, other energy sources like natural gas also release greenhouse gas emissions, increasing atmospheric gas concentrations.

Okay, let’s recap all this information. Beginning in the Industrial Revolution, coal started to be used as fuel for machines, and burning coal released byproducts such as CO2. CO2 is a greenhouse gas that can trap heat within the atmosphere, leading to rising global temperatures on average. Other fuel sources like natural gas can contribute more to rising emissions. Scientists have used extensive weather station samples, statistical analyses, other data collection methods, and computational models to arrive at this conclusion.

So temperatures on average may be rising globally, but this doesn’t necessarily mean that all areas have had increased temperatures. This is true because temperature changes affect regions differently based on factors such as latitude, biome, and altitude. In fact, this is the reason why scientists have moved away from using the term “Global Warming” and are instead opting to use “Climate Change” to describe what’s happening; climate change encompasses more of the effects and outcomes that can arise from greenhouse gas emissions.
