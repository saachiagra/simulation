Simulation Explanations

Temperature Differences

Source (21) has interesting information about different variables can influence data results. The paper said that the "degree of change" caused by different thermometers and their locative relative to sunlight/shade "varies from country to country and there is no simple way to quantify the magnitude of any effect." Source (21) and (22) both say that the heat island effect hasn't drastically changed overall temperature data, justifying this by saying "urban areas are only 0.5% of the land area". Source (23) had some ranges for the difference in temperatures between urban and rural areas (1-7°F).

Heat-Trapping Gases

Some gases are better at trapping heat than others. Source (24) has the global warming potentials of different gases, which compares how well a gas can trap heat compared to carbon dioxide (which has a GWP of 1). I *tried* to reflect this difference by having the differences in area correspond to the differences in GWPs, but I had to make the radii much smaller (making the circle area smaller) so that the molecules would fit within the alloted simulation space.

Carbon Isotopes

I had to make the concentration of C-14 higher for ~educational purposes~. The ratio between C-12 and C-13 (989:11) is accurate for the atmosphere, but for plants the ratio is 991:9 rather than 994:6. Source (25) has the delta value for plants, and Source (26) has a delta-to-percentage converter formula and atmospheric carbon isotope concentrations.

Additional Write-Ups

History of Temperature

The history of measuring fluctuations in temperature goes all the way back to 1610, when Galileo made a thermoscope using a glass sphere containing alcohols each with different densities (3). The alcohols would rise and fall within the container based on the ambient temperature, but this device didn’t have any markings to determine the exact temperature, hence why it’s not considered a “thermometer” (3). In 1612, Santorio Santorio made a thermoscope calibrated to measure human temperature, and in 1654, Ferdinand II de’ Medici made a thermometer “to depend on the expansion and contraction of a liquid, which was independent of barometric pressure” (3).

The first set of continuous temperature readings from thermometers comes from the Central England Temperature Data Series, which started in 1659 (4). Though, we don’t have substantial data 🐇 on global temperatures before the 1880s.

We may not have adequate thermometer readings from before, but there are other methods scientists use to calculate premodern temperatures!

C-14 Radioactive Decay

C-14 can turn into a N-14 atom through the process of beta minus decay. Essentially, a neutron within the carbon atom transforms into a proton, releasing an electron and an antineutrino in the process (5).
